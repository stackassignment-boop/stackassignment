Academic Integrity and
Compliance Guidelines

Everything you need to know about maintaining honesty, originality, and ethical conduct in your university studies — explained clearly and simply.

Academic integrity is the foundation of every genuine qualification. It means acting with honesty, fairness, trust, respect, and responsibility in all academic work. Universities treat breaches seriously — and understanding the rules is the first step to protecting your academic future.

Foundation

What Is Academic Integrity?

Academic integrity means that every piece of work you submit genuinely represents your own effort and thinking. It is not just about avoiding plagiarism — it encompasses every interaction you have with your studies, from how you reference sources to how you behave in exams.

Universities worldwide share a common commitment: that degrees must reflect real knowledge and effort. When integrity is upheld, your qualification carries real value and opens real doors.

The Six Core Values of Academic Integrity

Honesty

Always represent your work truthfully and accurately.

Fairness

Every student deserves equal standing through their own efforts.

Trust

Build confidence between you, your institution, and your peers.

Respect

Acknowledge the intellectual contributions of others properly.

Responsibility

Own your academic choices and their consequences fully.

Courage

Uphold these values even when it is difficult to do so.

Know the Rules

Types of Academic Misconduct

Academic misconduct covers a wide range of behaviours — some intentional, some unintentional. Whether deliberate or not, the consequences can be serious. Here are the most common types you need to be aware of:

Plagiarism

Using another person's ideas, words, or work without proper attribution. This includes direct copying, poor paraphrasing, missing citations, and copying from websites or textbooks — even if only a few paragraphs.

Self-Plagiarism

Resubmitting your own previously submitted work (from any course, at any institution) without permission from your tutor or coordinator. Each assessment must be fresh and original.

Contract Cheating

Paying, asking, or allowing someone else to write your assignment — including essay mills, online writing services, and friends or family. This applies whether or not any money changes hands.

Collusion

Working with other students on individual assessments without authorisation. This includes sharing notes, comparing answers, or submitting very similar work — even if you didn't intend to cheat.

Impersonation & Fraud

Pretending to be someone else in an exam, submitting fake medical certificates, fabricating references or data, or falsifying academic transcripts and email evidence.

Examination Misconduct

Bringing unauthorised materials into exams (including phones and smart watches), communicating during exams, copying from other students, or sharing exam questions and answers online.

Important: Intention Does Not Always Matter

Many institutions treat unintentional misconduct (such as poor referencing) as a serious matter, especially for students who have already received guidance. Ignorance of the rules is generally not accepted as a defence. Always double-check before submitting.

New Rules

Using AI Tools — What's Allowed?

Generative AI tools (including ChatGPT and similar platforms) are now explicitly addressed in university policies worldwide. Using AI to write or substantially alter your assessed work is treated as a form of contract cheating or plagiarism. Here's what you need to know:

Generative AI in Academic Work

✅ GENERALLY PERMITTED

  • Correcting spelling and punctuation
  • Basic grammar and syntax checks
  • Shortening long sentences (without changing meaning)
  • Formatting consistency (headers, footnotes)
  • Improving table or figure layouts

❌ NOT PERMITTED

  • Writing or rewriting arguments or ideas
  • Generating answers to assessment questions
  • Correcting or altering factual accuracy
  • Translating your work into another language
  • Reducing word count to meet a limit

Always Check Your Institution's Specific Rules

AI policies vary between universities and even between individual courses. Always read your assignment brief carefully. Some programmes may permit specific AI tools for certain tasks. When in doubt, ask your lecturer before using any AI assistance.

What Happens Next

The Investigation Process

If a suspected breach is identified, universities follow a structured and fair process. Here's what typically happens:

From Concern to Resolution — Step by Step

1

Concern Raised

A lecturer, invigilator, or automated tool (such as plagiarism detection software) identifies a potential breach. The concern is formally reported, typically within five working days of detection.

2

Formal Notification

You are notified in writing of the allegation, given a copy of the relevant policy, and provided with all evidence being considered. You are usually given at least five working days' notice before any meeting.

3

Investigation Meeting

You are invited to respond to the allegation in person or in writing. You may bring a support person (not a legal professional) to the meeting. Your marks may be withheld while the investigation is ongoing.

4

Decision & Outcome

A determination is made based on the available evidence. If misconduct is confirmed, a proportionate penalty is applied. The outcome is communicated to you in writing, normally within five working days of the decision.

5

Right of Appeal

If you disagree with the decision, you have the right to appeal — usually within 10 working days of being notified. Valid grounds include procedural irregularity, bias, disproportionate penalty, or new evidence not previously available.

Your Rights During an Investigation

You are entitled to be heard fairly. You can present mitigating circumstances, bring a support person, and request access to all evidence. No penalty is applied before the investigation is complete, and you are presumed innocent until misconduct is proven.

Consequences

Possible Penalties

Penalties are determined by the nature and severity of the misconduct, prior history, intent, and the impact on others. They range from educational guidance for minor issues to expulsion for serious, deliberate breaches.

Severity Typical Scenarios Possible Penalties
Minor Poor referencing, accidental paraphrasing, first-time unintentional errors Advisory letter, mark reduction, guidance from tutor
Moderate Significant copying, collusion, repeated poor practice Zero for assessment, capped resubmission, formal warning on record
Serious Contract cheating, impersonation, deliberate fraud, exam misconduct Fail grade, suspension, expulsion, degree revocation, legal referral

Degree Revocation Is Possible

In serious cases, misconduct discovered even after graduation can result in your degree being revoked. Universities may also notify employers, placement providers, or professional regulatory bodies of confirmed misconduct findings.
